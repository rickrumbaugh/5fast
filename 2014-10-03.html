The combination of big data and the Internet of Things is poised to transform the factory floor

Extracting data during the manufacturing process and implementing it in useful ways was definitely on the minds of those at Connected Enterprise. “All manufacturing plants would be into industrial automation,” says Paul McGreal, engineering manager at multinational medical device company Boston Scientific in Galway. “To survive in Ireland, you have to automate and innovate. But now we want to know how to get data from these machines.”
Already he can remotely access data from the manufacturing floor and see how a particular machine is working, which means he doesn’t have to be at his plant site. “Having the equipment connected to the internet, I can easily connect from home, do diagnostics, and fix it,” he says. “And I don’t have to have five or 10 engineers in there any more. Cisco could offer 50% upside by splitting into "fast growing" and "slow growing" businesses, according to one analyst

http://online.barrons.com/news/articles/SB51517841841143733463604580184141214517692

The markets Cisco sells into are massive and expanding, and its products are well-differentiated from the competition. But the businesses require heavy spending on research and development, which for now cuts into profits. Growth investors don't mind waiting for rapidly rising revenues to turn into future profits. Value investors would rather see the free cash flow today, with much of it spent on dividends.

By separating its fast-growth businesses from the rest, Cisco could attract both types of investors and unlock a higher price. A slow-growing "Cisco Solutions" business would generate heaps of cash and pay handsome dividends while protecting margins by reducing costs and buying out mature, cash-generating rivals. Based on peer valuations, it would likely sell for between three and 3.3 times revenue, or $22 to $24 a share, according to Sue.

The fast-growing "Cisco Cloud" business would focus its acquisition strategy on early-stage growth companies. It could fetch 4.5 to five times revenue, or $9 to $11 a share. Cisco also sits on about $6 a share in cash and equivalents, after subtracting debt. All told, Sue estimates a spinoff could drive Cisco's value to $37 to $41 a share, or 47% to 63% higher.

The cone of silence may finally be ready to descend on airline seats.  Yes!  Faster, please.

The noise cancellation process of the Quiet BubbleTM is similar to the noise cancelling headphones some travellers lug around, but Silentium claims the cutting-edge patented technology it developed can generate more than 10dB(A) noise reduction “across the entire audio spectrum” and has special algorithms that help the microchip, which generates the invisible bubble of calm, better predict the noise it needs to cancel out.

Silentium has already developed the microchip for the automotive sector and has recently introduced the product to aviation at the Airline Passenger Experience Expo (APEX) in Anaheim.

Silentium’s CPO, Yoel Naor, tells us that the company is already working with aviation suppliers and OEMs to integrate the chip into aircraft seats of In-Flight Entertainment equipment. The system can be embedded in the seat for active noise reduction, or to the public address system, cancelling out ambient noise in the cabin, or embedded into the In-Flight Entertainment. “We had project with an airplane seat manufacturer,” he says, “after the APEX , we will have some more partners that wish to implement it in VIP rooms, private jets and so on. But the main target is to bring it to the Economy class.”
